Young Voter Turnout Is On the Rise. Here's How to Vote in the Midterms as a College Student

Story by: Christine Loughran

October 18, 2022

College students who are registered to vote are more likely to end up casting a ballot, a study from Tufts University suggests.

In 2020, 80 percent of college students nationwide who were registered followed through on Election Day, an 11 percent increase from 2016. During the same period, voter turnout rates among eligible students at private colleges and universities spiked 14-17 percent. These trends were consistent and even higher at Manhattan College in the Bronx, where 59.6 of eligible students voted — an 18 percent increase in turnout compared to 2016. This success is partly owed to institutional “get out the vote” initiatives that were led by a voter engagement committee that shared its messages on social media and organized voter registration tabling events on campus. A partnership with the women’s basketball coach also yielded a 100 percent voter registration rate among student-athletes on that team.

To ensure New York City college students vote in the upcoming midterm election on November 8, re-energizing 2020 voters is essential, as is ramping up efforts to register newly eligible students. Then, be sure they cast their vote on Election Day. College students, voting is an effective way for policymakers to address the issues you care about. Don’t wait to register — you must apply by October 14 to participate in the midterms.

Follow this step-by-step guide to have your voice heard in the November midterm elections:

Step 1: Register to Vote

You may register to vote using the address of your family’s home or where you attend school. Just be sure to change your home address whenever it changes. New York’s voter registration form requires the New York State DMV number on your driver’s license. or the last four digits of your Social Security number. Register here.

Step 2: Request an Absentee Ballot

So, you’re already registered but at a different address. No problem – you may request an absentee ballot if you won’t be in your county of registration on Election Day. Request an absentee ballot online and return it by mail or in person by the time polls close on Election Day (that means you can hand in your absentee ballot when you go to vote).

Step 3: Learn About the Candidates

Head to Vote411.org and enter the address you’ll be voting from in November to learn more about the races happening in your voting district, which candidates are on the ballot and what they stand for.

Step 4: Show up on Voting Day

Locate the polling place where you’re assigned to vote and make a plan for getting there. Tip: use Google Maps to determine which New York City buses and/or subways you’ll need to take and if you have class, leave yourself enough time!
